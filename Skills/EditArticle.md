A great article edit is not a line-by-line polish — it's a structural intervention followed by prose refinement. The structure determines whether the article is coherent. The prose determines whether it's readable. Fix structure first, or you're polishing something that doesn't hold together.

## Editing Mindset

**You are an editor, not a ghostwriter:**

- Preserve the author's voice — improve clarity, don't homogenize
- Separate structural problems (order, gaps, redundancy) from prose problems (clarity, length, word choice)
- Every section should earn its place — cut mercilessly
- Information has dependencies: don't explain X before the reader has Y

**The editor's contract:**

- Be specific — "this is unclear" is not feedback
- Explain WHY a change improves the article
- Preserve meaning — never change what the author is saying without flagging it
- Respect the author's register (formal/casual, technical/general)

---

## Process

### Step 1: Structural analysis

Read the article completely before touching anything.

Divide it into sections based on headings (or logical breaks if there are no headings).

Think of information as a directed acyclic graph — each piece of information may depend on other pieces. Map the dependencies:

- What does the reader need to know first?
- What can only be understood after reading X?
- What is repeated or covered in multiple sections?
- What is missing that the reader will need?

Identify:

- Sections that are in the wrong order
- Sections that can be merged (same concept, split unnecessarily)
- Sections that should be split (multiple distinct ideas crammed together)
- Gaps (concepts referenced before being explained)
- Redundancy (same information in multiple places)

### Step 2: Confirm structure with the author

Present the proposed section order and structure. Explain the reasoning for any reordering.

Ask:

- Does this ordering make sense to you?
- Is there anything I'm missing?
- Are there constraints on order I'm not aware of (e.g. "the intro must come first for SEO")?

Only proceed to prose editing once the structure is confirmed.

### Step 3: Rewrite section by section

For each section:

1. Identify the one thing this section needs to communicate
2. Cut anything that doesn't serve that one thing
3. Rewrite for clarity using a maximum of **240 characters per paragraph**
4. Ensure the section opens by orienting the reader (what are we about to learn?) and closes by bridging to the next section

**Prose rules:**

- Prefer active voice over passive
- Prefer concrete nouns over abstract ones
- Cut throat-clearing sentences ("In this section, we will discuss...")
- Cut hedging that weakens the point ("It might be worth considering...")
- One idea per sentence when the idea is complex
- Short sentences after long ones — vary the rhythm

## Comment Conventions

When making non-obvious changes, annotate:

```
[RESTRUCTURED] Moved this section before X because it depends on Y.

[CUT] Removed this paragraph — it repeated what was said in the intro.

[MERGED] Combined these two sections — they were both explaining the same concept.

[REWRITTEN] Rewrote for clarity — the original was grammatically correct but required re-reading.

[QUESTION] I wasn't sure what this meant — I interpreted it as X. Is that right?
```

---

## Common Mistakes

**[CRITICAL]** Changing prose before fixing structure — you'll do work twice.

**[CRITICAL]** Changing the author's meaning without flagging it — always mark where you've interpreted something.

**[IMPORTANT]** Paragraphs over 240 characters — long paragraphs kill readability. Break them.

**[SUGGESTION]** Don't over-edit voice — the article should still sound like the author when you're done.

## Review Summary

**Overall:** Structure first, then prose.

**Must address before done:**

- All structural issues (order, gaps, redundancy) confirmed with author
- No paragraph exceeds 240 characters

**Well done when:**

- The article can be skimmed by reading only the first sentence of each paragraph
- Every section earns its place
